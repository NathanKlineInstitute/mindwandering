The Science

Your brain has slow rhythms you've never heard of

Think of brain activity like a symphony: rapid violin notes are your thoughts and reactions. But underneath, there's a slow bass drum — cycling over seconds and minutes — that sets the tempo for everything else.

We call these slow brain rhythms. They continuously shift you between two modes: focused on the outside world, or turned inward toward memories, plans, and daydreams.

When these rhythms break down, the consequences may be profound — contributing to ADHD, depression, anxiety, and schizophrenia.

For Scientists
Overarching Hypothesis

Slow fluctuations (<1 Hz) are critical determinants of cognition and behavior

This Conte Center tests the hypothesis that slow fluctuations of neural activity extending below 1 Hz, down to at least 0.01 Hz (timescales of seconds to 10s of seconds) are critical determinants of affect, cognition, and behavior.

Linking Hypothesis 1

Local slow neural fluctuations link to larger-scale Slow Brain Network Fluctuations (SBNFs) that significantly impact cognitive processes (attention, RT), behavioral indices, and autonomic measures (HRV, pupil diameter).

Linking Hypothesis 2

There is hierarchical phase-amplitude coupling (PAC) of neural activity across frequency ranges — the phase of lower-frequency activity controls the amplitude of higher-frequency activity, integrating across timescales.

Why It Matters

From slow rhythms to everyday life — and mental health

Scientific significance and translational relevance
🎯

Why focus naturally ebbs and flows

Slow rhythms continuously cycle the brain between focused attention and mind-wandering. Understanding this may reveal why concentration is hard for some people — and how to help.

Attentional sampling and SBNFs

SBNFs gate fronto-parietal network engagement through PAC, driving rhythmic fluctuations in perceptual sensitivity and behavioral performance across seconds-to-minutes timescales.

💭

Why mind-wandering is actually useful

Off-task thought supports creativity, long-term planning, and emotional processing. SBNFs orchestrate when and how the brain productively turns inward.

Default mode network regulation

SBNFs drive alternation between task-positive and default mode network dominance. PAC mediates the temporal structure of DMN activation and internally-directed cognition.

💙

Mental health connections

Disrupted slow rhythms are implicated in ADHD, depression, and schizophrenia. Our research may point toward new treatments that target these underlying rhythmic mechanisms.

Neuropsychiatric translational goals

Disrupted SBNFs are linked to ADHD, MDD, and schizophrenia. The Center aims to develop mechanistic understanding sufficient to propose novel neuromodulation-based interventions.

⚡

Brain and body are in constant dialogue

Slow brain rhythms are linked to heart rate, breathing, and pupil dilation. Understanding this brain–body connection opens new doors for understanding arousal and mental states.

Autonomic-neural coupling

SBNFs co-vary with cardiac variability, pupillometry, electrodermal activity, and respiration. Project 1 simultaneously records all modalities to map these relationships multimodally.

🔬

Studying the brain at every scale

We study these rhythms from single brain cells all the way to whole-brain networks — bridging scales in a way that no single approach alone can achieve.

Multi-scale integrative approach

fMRI/EEG (macroscale) → iEEG/ECoG (mesoscale) → laminar LFP/MUA (microscale) → biophysical modeling bridges all levels, with cross-species alignment via Core B.

🌐

Open science for everyone

All datasets are freely shared with researchers worldwide. Our data-sharing infrastructure means discoveries reach other scientists — and patients — as quickly as possible.

FAIR data principles and open sharing

Core C implements FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) data management. All processed datasets are released via INDI/NKI infrastructure following quality control by Core C.

Research Program

Four projects. One integrative vision.

Our center brings together human brain scanning, direct brain recordings, animal studies, and computer modeling — all focused on the same slow rhythms.

Four research projects + two support cores

Combining fMRI/EEG in humans, iEEG in surgical epilepsy patients, laminar electrophysiology in NHPs, and biophysical modeling — linked by multimodal alignment (Core B) and FAIR data sharing (Core C).
Project 1 · Child Mind Institute / NKI

Macroscale Physiology & Cognitive Correlates

We invite healthy adults for simultaneous brain scans (MRI) and brainwave recordings (EEG) while they perform tasks or freely daydream. We also track their heart rate, breathing, and pupil size — and periodically ask "What were you just thinking about?" to capture the contents of internal thought.

Simultaneous fMRI/EEG + autonomic measures (ECG, EDA, pupillometry, respiration, EMG) across tasks ranging from unstructured rest to structured cognitive paradigms. Experience sampling probes internal cognition. Pharmacological manipulation of arousal (Aim 2b).

Project 2 · UC Berkeley

Meso-Microscale Physiology via Intracranial EEG

Some epilepsy patients already have electrodes placed directly on their brain as part of their medical care. With their consent, we record during three types of sessions — structured tasks, movie watching, and multi-day free experience — to see how slow rhythms govern shifts between internal and external thought.

iEEG in surgical epilepsy patients. Three conditions of increasing naturalism: structured attention-switching tasks, passive movie viewing (EOMs/pupillometry/alpha as SBNF indices), and multi-day continuous experience sampling. Focus on DMN–FPCN interactions; BHA and SUA as cortical activation indices.

Project 3 · Nathan Kline Institute

Multiscale Physiology & Causal Mechanisms

Using macaque monkeys — whose brain organization closely mirrors ours — we record from individual brain cells using tiny electrode arrays, then use targeted drugs and electrical stimulation to test which brain circuits actually cause the slow rhythms we observe in humans.

NHP laminar multielectrode + brush array recordings across thalamocortical and AIC systems. Simultaneous scalp EEG/fMRI. Causal manipulation: methylphenidate pharmacology, vagus nerve stimulation, and reversible chemogenetic (DREADD) inactivation of AIC→ACC/NB pathways (Aim 2).

CS
Charles Schroeder, PhD
Nathan Kline Institute
Project 4 · Brown University

Integrated Network & Cell-Circuit Modeling

We build detailed computer simulations of brain circuits — virtual brains that generate the same rhythms as real ones. By tweaking the model, we can test theories about what causes slow rhythms without ever needing an electrode. The models also make predictions that guide new experiments.

Two-level modeling: (1) dynamical systems/large-scale network models fit to EEG/fMRI/s-EEG capturing bistability and CAP states; (2) biophysically detailed neocortical microcircuit models (laminar resolution) bridging to NHP LFP/CSD. Hybrid multi-scale model integrating both levels (Aim 3).

Not at all — in fact, it may be essential. Research shows mind-wandering supports long-term planning, creativity, emotional processing, and memory consolidation. The problem arises when the brain gets stuck in one mode. A healthy brain flexibly shifts between focused attention and internal thought.
Your brain generates electrical signals constantly. Slow brain rhythms are waves that cycle over seconds or even minutes — far slower than the well-known alpha or gamma waves. They're like the ocean tide rather than surface ripples. Our research focuses on these very slow waves and how they organize all faster brain activity like a conductor.
In many neuropsychiatric conditions, the brain's ability to smoothly transition between focused and wandering states appears disrupted. In ADHD, slow rhythms may fail to properly suppress the default mode network during tasks. In depression, the brain may be stuck in ruminative inward focus. By understanding the rhythms that regulate these transitions, we hope to find new treatment targets.

Center Aims

Three center-wide specific aims

AIM 1

Define cognitive, behavioral & autonomic correlates of macroscale SBNFs in humans

Simultaneous fMRI/EEG with multimodal autonomic recording across tasks; pharmacological manipulation of network fluctuations (Project 1).

AIM 2

Inform physiological interpretation via meso- and microscale measures

iEEG in humans (Project 2) + NHP laminar recordings + causal neuromodulation (Project 3) bridge macroscale fMRI findings to cellular mechanisms.

AIM 3

Develop iterative modeling–empirical interactions to bridge data scales

Network and cell-circuit models updated iteratively with empirical data from Projects 1–3; hybrid multi-scale model integrates both (Project 4).
