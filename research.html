For Everyone

The core question: why do slow brain rhythms matter?

We study the very slow waves in brain activity — cycles of seconds to minutes — that quietly direct when you focus, when you daydream, and how effectively your brain processes the world. Our research spans human brain scanning, recordings from the brains of epilepsy patients (with their consent), studies in monkeys, and computer modeling.

THE BIG IDEA

Slow brain rhythms are not background noise — they are a fundamental organizing principle of brain function that shapes cognition, emotion, and behavior moment to moment.

THE GOAL

To understand slow rhythms well enough to develop new treatments for neuropsychiatric disorders where these rhythms go wrong — including ADHD, depression, and schizophrenia.

Overarching Hypothesis

Slow fluctuations of neural activity <1 Hz (down to ≥0.01 Hz) are critical determinants of affect, cognition, and behavior.

Linking Hypothesis 1

Local slow neural fluctuations link to large-scale SBNFs that significantly impact attention, RT, and autonomic measures (HRV, pupil).

Linking Hypothesis 2

Hierarchical PAC — the phase of low-frequency activity controlling amplitude of higher frequencies — integrates brain function across timescales.

Plain language

Mapping the full picture of slow brain rhythms in people

What we're asking: How do slow brain rhythms relate to what you're thinking, how you're feeling, and how well you perform? We track everything — brain activity, body signals, and inner thoughts — simultaneously.

In this project, healthy adults visit our lab for brain scans and brainwave recordings while doing everything from complex cognitive tasks to simply resting with their eyes closed. Every few minutes, we pause and ask them: "What were you just thinking about?" — capturing the actual contents of their internal thoughts.

At the same time, we measure heart rate, breathing, pupil size, skin response, and muscle activity. This lets us build a rich, simultaneous picture of how slow brain rhythms relate to all these signals — and to the contents of ongoing thought.

We also use a controlled drug study (with appropriate safeguards) to investigate whether increasing arousal — temporarily — changes how slow rhythms operate, which would tell us whether arousal is a cause or consequence of those rhythms.

Scientific abstract

Macroscale physiology and functional correlates of slow network fluctuations

Overarching goal: Broadly characterize the relationship of slow fluctuations across multiple simultaneous measures of brain activity, physiology, behavior, and cognition to understand their role in orchestrating externally vs. internally directed attention.

Slow fluctuations in behavioral, cognitive, and neural states are an omnipresent feature of the brain across species and are aberrant in multiple disorders of the nervous system. SBNFs critically organize behavior across extended timescales: as drifts in arousal over minutes, as well as the ability to switch between attending externally versus turning internally to plan future actions and dwell on recent experiences.

This project performs a deep characterization of SBNFs by collecting extensive multimodal neural and physiological recordings (simultaneous fMRI, EEG, EDA, pupillometry, respiration, ECG, EMG) in healthy human participants across tasks ranging from unstructured rest to highly structured attention-demanding paradigms. Experience sampling assesses the contents of ongoing cognition throughout.

Specific Aims

  • Aim 1 Deep characterization of SBNFs through simultaneous multimodal neural and physiological recordings across an extended array of tasks, mapping slow fluctuations to externally-oriented task performance and the contents of internal cognition.
  • Aim 2a Directly assess contributions of SBNFs to internally-oriented cognition (learning, goal planning tasks) and measure neural/behavioral markers of continued internal processing and trade-offs between internally- vs. externally-oriented modes.
  • Aim 2b Assess causal contributions of cognitive control and arousal to SBNFs, using task demands and double-blind pharmacological manipulation (methylphenidate) to dissociate their distinct contributions.

Plain language

A window inside the human brain — with consent from epilepsy patients

What we're asking: When your mind wanders away from the task at hand, what is actually happening inside your brain's circuitry? Epilepsy patients who already have brain electrodes placed for medical reasons give us a unique chance to find out.

Some patients with epilepsy have electrodes temporarily placed directly on their brain's surface to locate seizure foci. With their consent, these electrodes let us record brain activity at a level of detail impossible from outside the skull — like hearing individual instruments instead of a whole orchestra through a wall.

We run three types of sessions, from most structured to most natural: focused attention tasks, passive movie watching (where minds naturally drift), and multi-day continuous recording paired with experience sampling probes. This lets us see exactly what the brain does as it transitions between externally-focused and internally-focused states.

Scientific abstract

Meso-microscale physiology and dynamics of slow network fluctuations

Core approach: Use iEEG to address the role of SBNFs and local cortical activation in neural circuits supporting external attention and attention to internal thoughts — focusing on DMN and FPCN interactions across three experimental conditions of increasing naturalism.

Neural networks orchestrate behavior across multiple timescales and are disordered in numerous developmental, neurological, and psychiatric disorders. This project uses intracranial EEG to examine differential interactions between the default mode network (DMN) and the fronto-parietal attention networks (FPCN) supporting externally vs. internally directed attention. Local neural activation is measured with broadband high-frequency activity (BHA; 70–200 Hz) and single-unit activity (SUA). Inter-areal network interactions are assessed using phase slope index, single-trial HFA onsets, Granger causality, directional PAC, representational similarity analysis, and mutual information.

Specific Aims

  • Aim 1 Structured tasks with attention switching between external and internal states — focus on anterior insula and posterior cingulate in network switching; intermodal auditory-visual paradigm probes attention and lapses.
  • Aim 2 Naturalistic movie-viewing paradigm — more ecological conditions entailing passive viewing with EOMs, alpha oscillations, BHA, and pupillometry indexing drifts from active attention to internal thought. Post-movie recognition tests confirm attention lapses.
  • Aim 3 Multi-day experience sampling coupled with continuous iEEG recording — subjects queried about their internal mental state (on-task, off-task, mind-wandering) classified as freely moving thoughts, deliberately constrained thoughts, or automatically constrained thoughts.

Plain language

Finding the cellular machinery behind slow brain rhythms

What we're asking: Which brain circuits actually generate slow rhythms — and can we change them? Brain scans can tell us where slow rhythms occur, but not how they arise from individual cells and circuits. We use animal studies to answer that question.

We work with macaque monkeys, whose brains are organized very similarly to human brains. Using tiny electrode arrays, we can record from individual brain cells and local circuits simultaneously, at the same time as performing the equivalent of a brain scan.

Crucially, this project goes beyond description: we use targeted drugs, controlled electrical stimulation, and a technique called chemogenetics (which uses engineered proteins to temporarily switch off specific pathways) to test whether particular circuits are truly the cause of slow rhythms — not just correlated with them. This causal understanding is essential for developing treatments.

Scientific abstract

Multiscale physiology and causal mechanisms of slow network fluctuations

Core goal: Advance the physiological interpretation of macroscale findings using meso- and microscale measures in humans and NHPs — defining SBNFs across distributed brain areas and identifying the cellular and circuit mechanisms that generate and modulate them.

NHP work provides simultaneous scalp EEG/fMRI, behavioral-cognitive, and autonomic data under three common tasks and conditions: intermodal attention, movie watching, and rest. Brush and laminar array recordings define microscopic cell circuits and physiological processes (Aim 1). A second aim identifies processes and structures triggering and modulating SBNF dynamics via pharmacology and neuromodulation (Aim 2).

Specific Aims

  • Aim 1 Link macroscale measures of P1 meso- and microscale physiology of SBNFs — defining SBNFs across distributed brain areas including the interoceptive system (AIC) and thalamocortical auditory system, testing hypotheses about opposing unit/LFP activation levels in task-positive vs. task-negative networks.
  • Aim 2 Define processes and structures triggering/modulating SBNFs: (a) pharmacologically increase arousal via methylphenidate as direct link to P1; (b) vagus nerve stimulation (impacts cholinergic/dopaminergic systems and interoceptive network); (c) reversible chemogenetic (DREADD) inactivation of AIC projections to ACC and nucleus basalis to test causal roles of these pathways.

Plain language

Building a computer model of the brain's slow rhythms

What we're asking: Can we build a virtual brain accurate enough to reproduce the slow rhythms we observe — and use it to test theories too complex to test in experiments alone?

Our computational team builds two types of models that work together. The first is a large-scale network model — like a map of the whole brain — that captures how slow rhythm activity propagates across brain regions. The second is a biophysically detailed circuit model — zoomed in to a small patch of cortex — that simulates what individual neurons are actually doing.

These models don't just describe what we observe — they allow us to test mechanistic theories. If we change a synaptic parameter or remove a specific cell type in the model, does the slow rhythm disappear? If so, that becomes a testable prediction for the animal experiments in Project 3. This back-and-forth between models and experiments accelerates discovery.

Scientific abstract

Integrated brain network and cell-circuit models of slow network fluctuations

Core goal: Develop iterative interactions between modeling and empirical studies to integrate knowledge across data scales — synthesizing multi-modal recordings from Projects 1–3 into multi-scale mechanistic computational models of cortical dynamics and SBNFs.

Project 4 develops novel computational models of neural circuit dynamics and applies them to fit features of multi-modal neural recordings in Projects 1–3. Models are used to test hypotheses and gain insight into dynamical and biophysical mechanisms underlying SBNFs and their impact on local circuit processing of sensory information.

Specific Aims

  • Aim 1 Fit a dynamical systems model to capture spectral state dynamics in cortical regions as measured by LFP, EEG, iEEG. Test hypothesis that slow (~0.1–1 Hz) fluctuations in spectral state can be captured through bistability — transitions induced by noise and adaptation. Large-scale network model simulates brain-wide phenomena (fMRI functional connectivity, CAP states).
  • Aim 2 Develop biophysically detailed models of neocortical circuits with laminar resolution — designed to bring macroscale human iEEG/EEG to microscale phenomena (cell spiking, LFP/CSD). Test how cell type-specific properties and thalamocortical connectivity must be combined to replicate multiscale dynamics. Systematically explore PAC mechanisms underlying SBNFs.
  • Aim 3 (Exploratory) Develop a multi-scale model embedding biophysically detailed microcircuit models from Aim 2 as distinct nodes in a large-scale network in which other nodes are simulated as phenomenological dynamical systems from Aim 1.

Making sure our data is integrated and shared openly

Research Support Cores B & C

CORE B · RS-001 (P926)

Multimodal Data Analysis & Integration

TX
Ting Xu, PhD
Child Mind Institute · Vanderbilt

Our data comes in many forms — brain scans from humans, electrical recordings from monkeys, and more. Core B builds the mathematical tools that put all this data into a common framework so scientists can meaningfully compare findings across studies and species.

Develops multimodal alignment frameworks establishing a common space for analysis across modalities, individuals, and species (Projects 1–3). Aims: (1) functional alignment framework for cross-modal comparison; (2) refine cross-species alignment using functional data from P1–2 and macaque data from P3; (3) state-space framework for decomposing brain dynamics into spatiotemporal states from joint EEG, fMRI, and physiological signals.

CORE C · RS-002 (P952)

Data Aggregation, Standardization & Sharing

AF
Alexandre Franco, PhD
Nathan Kline Institute

Every dataset we collect is carefully quality-checked, standardized, and made openly available to the scientific community worldwide — following FAIR principles so that any researcher can access and build on our work, accelerating discovery globally.

DASS-C is the component through which overall management and sharing of experimental and processed data is conducted. Follows FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) principles. Four aims: (1) guide and support community data standards development; (2) provide data management platform for aggregation and sharing; (3) perform QC and minimal preprocessing; (4) facilitate dataset dissemination and reproducibility of analyses.
